The History of Cycling in Singapore

POSTCARD FROM SINGAPORE - Leaders Lowdown - Justin Holmwood - Bike North. (2017). Retrieved February 18, 2022, from Bikenorth.org.au website: https://www.bikenorth.org.au/2018/06/06/postcard-from-singapore-leaders-lowdown-justin-holmwood/

The first bicycle-propelled rickshaw, also known as the trishaw, was introduced during the Japanese Occupation of Singapore between 1942 and 1945 (K. Vincent, 2014, paragraph 1). The trishaw evolved from the rickshaw, pulled by a man (Samuel, D. S., 2010 & Warren, J. F., 2003). Trishaws first appeared in Singapore in April 1914 as pedalled rickshaws, which were tricycles with chairs bolted to them. The unknown origin of the initial batch of 15 trishaws was crudely constructed and did not last long on the streets of Singapore. The British colonial authorities rejected an American company’s request to import 500 trishaws later that year due to considerations for road safety (Lim, J., 1995). These replaced the rickshaws and is a legacy in transport history for creating a leap. There is various non-motorised wheeled transport that has advanced with technology through development. They continue to be used as a tourist attraction in Singapore today. Bicycles became popular in the West in the 1890s, but they were expensive in Asia and few. (Tan, B. 2013)



It is unclear when bicycles were introduced to Singapore, but it was believed to be the same time as the trishaw. Japan developed its bicycle manufacturing industry, and cycling became more common in Asia including Singapore. By the 1960s, cycling was a very common and the most used type of transportation by people to move from place to place in Singapore. (Pg 41 paragraph 3, Ping, K. P., & Diew , W. Y., 2012) In the 1970s, bicycle usage started to drop drastically in Singapore when car and motorcycle ownership rose quickly. Low-class forms of travel were walking, cycling and public transport compared to private vehicles. Many organisations and companies started to stray away from manufacturing parts or bicycles and focusing on motor vehicles instead. Most of the cycling tracks were removed to widen roads and build more roads and infrastructure for cars and neglect the infrastructure that cyclists need for them to cycle. In recent years, more people have been more conscious about the environment as well as health, hence the trend of cycling has risen again.

3 main groups of cyclist in Singapore (Who's Riding? 2021):

Neighborhood Cyclist(for intra-town travel)

Bike Trail Cyclist (Intermediate Cyclists who ride on curated trails)

Road Cyclist(Mostly experienced cyclists who participate actively in cycling events)

Many novice cyclists tend to be less skilful hence they would need more space in order for them to cycle and control themselves which can prevent crashes with others. Hence it is important they get enough space. (Pg 70, Libby 2022)


Since many people use cycling as a way to travel to transport nodes like MRT stations There are already 3,000 bicycle racks at 32 MRT stations since 2011, The ministry of transport plans to add 500 new racks at 11 MRT stations by 2014. (MOT 2019)


The first effort made towards a more cycling-friendly city began in 1992 when the Singapore government decided to build a 300km Cycling network also known as the Park Connector Network or PCN. It would cost about 43 million dollars to construct. A year after, it was announced by the government agencies of a National Cycling Plan consisting of the earlier project, it aims to have 700km of cycling paths including park connectors by 2030. As of 2019, more than half of the 700km national cycling plan has already been completed(The Big Read 2019). In 2020, the URA set out a new goal to have up to 1320km of cycling paths and park connectors by 2030(Hermes Auto 2020). Although it is against the law for cyclists to cycle on pedestrian paths, many still do it due to the fact that it is very comprehensive and safer than riding on the road. The PCN aims to connect parks as well as transport modes together so that commuters can use the path for last mile and first mile connections and leisure activities

Proposed Idea

Through the use of improved infrastructure, users of bikes would be much more enticed and encouraged to commute to their palace of work or study through the use of biking. It would be seen as a win-win situation for both motorists, pedestrians and cyclists. As more people cycling means that there are less people craving on the road. We do hope that future roads that are being developed do include a cycling path for cyclists especially along routes between residential areas and workplaces such as factories and offices, reduce the speed limit of vehicles so cycling paths can be feasible on these roads, and decrease the number of shared paths. As Singapore continues to build and make more cycling paths, bridges and tunnels and improve existing paths by prioritising pedestrians and cyclists over motorists. We want to help solve one of the reasons why Singaporeans do not wish to cycle, which is the hot and unpredictable weather. Our solution can provide a shelter so whether is it the weather is raining or too hot people could rest in the shelter for a break. This would elevate the rider experience by giving them a better quality of comfort and weather protection. Which is one of the fundamentals of active mobility design. This also allows cyclists to stop and take a short break from their journey before continuing to make their way to their destinations. This rest stop will be equipped with refreshments, seating areas and vending machines, for cyclists to cool themselves down.
