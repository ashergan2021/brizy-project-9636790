Challenge

Our challenge is Singapore's hot weather when cycling. Many people are discouraged from taking the PMD as a form of transport because of the unpredictable weather, causing many commuters to be wary of taking the form of transportation. Through improved infrastructure, bikes would be more enticed and encouraged to commute to their place of work or study through biking. It would be a win-win situation for motorists, pedestrians, and cyclists as more people would choose to cycle, meaning fewer people crave on the road. We hope that the future developed roads includes cycling paths for cyclists, especially along routes between residential areas and workplaces such as factories and offices. Other than that, we hope the future laws would reduce the speed limit of vehicles so cycling paths can be feasible on these roads and decrease the number of shared pathways. Many current cycling paths are narrow and shared with pedestrians, and some may not be a continuous route for long journeys. Cyclists then have to opt for a different option which takes more time to travel further for a smooth pathway and a safer journey. Singapore continues to build and make more cycling paths, bridges and tunnels and improve existing routes by prioritising pedestrians and cyclists over motorists and car drivers. These connectors will enable a more seamless journey for cyclists to travel to their destinations with ease.


We are trying to encourage PMD and Cycling, however, Singapore's hot weather discourages people from using it as a form of transport that is used often. We conducted a survey to find out how willing Singaporeans are to choose the bike as an option to travel to their location. The responses from our form show that 68.3% of the respondents travel 12km or less for their daily commute. This proves that there is a large percentage of people who could reach their destination by cycling for 30 minutes or less. 80.5% of respondents also showed that the main reason for them not choosing to cycle to their destinations was due to weather conditions, which was the highest chosen option out of the 7 options. An example was that the temperature during the day was too high. The majority of the respondents felt that providing better infrastructure for people to rest in between or after their cycling journey would be able to encourage more people to cycle. This further supported that through improving infrastructure, people could hide from the hot weather or occasional rains and would be able to encourage them to cycle.

Solution

Our solution is to take a rest stop that is convenient and made for cyclists which can also provide shelter from the hot and unpredictable weather. The rest stop would have a path that diverges from the main cycling path. The diverged path would circle around the rest area, the outer side of the path would be the bicycle parking area with stands for people to lock their bicycles too. The path would make a full round around the rest stop and link back to the main path. When the path diverges at the entrance, there would be conveniently placed vending machines to buy things(food, drinks, sunglasses etc) on the go and mist fans that would activate when the temperature is hot. The cyclists can then conveniently join back the main path. This would elevate the rider's experience by giving them a better quality of comfort and weather protection, one of the fundamentals of functional mobility design. The rest stop would be unmanned and would have amenities such as toilets, rest areas, more vending machines, Area Directory, water coolers and rubbish bins. The rest area would also act as a shelter for people in case of unfavourable weather conditions such as rain. The rest stop would also have fans and mist fans to cool down the cyclists in the hot weather.


We have also come up with an app that is compatible with all devices, "Krista", which includes a QR code scanner, counting the number of QR codes one can scan at the multiple shelters provided along the journey. The app would then transfer the QR codes into points, which you can exchange for the different grocery brand or Health Promotion Board (HBP) vouchers, and redeem them to use them. Commuters can also make use of the points by claiming their wanted cycling gears in vending machines at the shelters. The vending machine has a wide variety of different gears for cyclists to choose from, from bicycle helmets to knee guards. Once they have picked their desired one, the vending machine would dispense the equipment. Points can be accumulated, so, the more points you have, the better the gift you can claim. We will also leverage off existing technology developed to add in a feature that can predict the weather with high accuracy, so the user knows when to cycle and when not to. The app also shows the closest bicycle storage area to where they are standing at, enabling them to park their bike safely at the storage areas shown in the app. The app will also be outfitted with a counter to state how many spots there are left at the point. Other than the above points, the app can also inform people on the road conditions of certain routes, allowing them to be notified when their usual path is blocked with constructions, road accidents, and give alternative routes which they can take to reach their destination with ease. The app will also show where all the shelters are located, for users to find the closes area to seek shelter from the weather or to take a short break from the long trip. It can also track the distance the cyclists has cycled, and cyclists can set a targeted distance they would like to travel on the app, which will send reminders of how much further they would have to travel to hit their goal. Virtual events will also be set up, rewarding users that complete the event with vouchers or a virtual model that can be put up.


There are currently apps that serve a similar purpose in Singapore. However, our team has successfully identified each of the app’s flaws; Strava, an activity recording app, which can be uploaded to Strava feed, where your friends and followers can share their own races and workouts, give kudos to great performances and leave comments on each other's activities. It has no rewards system. LumiHealth focuses on the individual's holistic health and wellness, including physical activity, mental wellbeing, sleep, nutrition, preventive screening, and vaccinations, plus regular updates on managing through the COVID-19 pandemic, however they do not have a navigation system built-in by fault. Through the faults that we have identified, we came together to build a solution that brings essential core features as well as reward systems into place. This will allow Singaporeans to maintain a sustainable healthy lifestyle and one step closer to a carbon-neutral Singapore.

How solution resolved challenge:

The solution provides shelter from weather, food, water, and amenities for cyclists. They are able to catch their breath, take a break, buy something to consume at the rest stop. The amenities allow people to take a break from cycling for reasons such as hunger, needing to go to the toilet and many more.

This solution also includes a mobile-app deliverable as well as the ability for it to be synced and used interchanged with our vending machines. Through this additional application of technology, people would be much more incentivised to stay healthy and contribute to a carbon neutral Singapore due to the convinces it offers as well as a monetary benefit.
